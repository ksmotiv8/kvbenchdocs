**OPINION AND ORDER**  
  **RULING ON THE CONSTITUTIONALITY OF A STATE LAW RESTRICTING FREEDOM OF SPEECH IN PUBLIC SPACES**

**Case No. 2:22-cv-01435-RCD**  
**Date: April 12, 2025**

---

**I. INTRODUCTION**

This Court has been presented with a critical question of constitutional law concerning the scope and reach of the First Amendment in the context of public space regulation. The plaintiff, Citizens for Free Expression, Inc., a nonprofit organization dedicated to the protection and promotion of free speech in public forums, challenges the constitutionality of a state law enacted by the State of Nevada, entitled *Nevada Public Peace Act of 2023* (hereinafter “the Act”), which imposes restrictions on the expression of certain viewpoints in public spaces. The defendants, the Department of Public Safety and the Nevada Public Peace Commission, assert that the Act is a legitimate exercise of the state’s police power and serves a compelling governmental interest in maintaining public order and safety.

This Court has conducted a thorough examination of the facts, legal arguments, and constitutional principles at stake. In doing so, it has considered the text of the Act, the statutory framework in which it operates, the historical and doctrinal precedents guiding this Court’s interpretation of the First Amendment, and the factual circumstances under which the Act has been applied. This opinion constitutes the Court’s determination of the constitutional validity of the Act under the First Amendment to the United States Constitution, as incorporated by the Fourteenth Amendment.

---

**II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND**

The Act, enacted in 2023, is a comprehensive legislative measure that modifies the existing legal framework governing public expression in Nevada. Section 1 of the Act, entitled “Purpose and Policy,” states that the purpose of the legislation is “to preserve public order, protect public safety, and ensure that public spaces remain accessible for all citizens.” Section 2 of the Act, titled “Definitions,” sets forth the scope of “public spaces” to include “any area of land or property owned, leased, or controlled by the State of Nevada, or any entity thereof, that is open to the public for recreational, commercial, or governmental purposes.”

Section 3 of the Act, which forms the operative provision of the law, restricts the use of public spaces for the dissemination of “controversial or disruptive content.” Specifically, it prohibits the distribution of any materials or the delivery of any speech that is “likely to provoke immediate and substantial disruption of public order or safety.” The Act further empowers the Nevada Public Peace Commission to issue “public peace directives” to local jurisdictions, directing them to enforce the Act’s restrictions on the basis of an assessment of “imminent threat to public order.”

The plaintiff, Citizens for Free Expression, Inc., alleges that the Act violates the First Amendment by imposing content-based restrictions on speech without a sufficient governmental interest. The plaintiff argues that the Act is overbroad, as it allows for the suppression of speech based on the content of the message rather than the context in which it is spoken. The plaintiff further contends that the Act is not narrowly tailored to serve a significant governmental interest, as the restrictions are not limited to speech that poses an imminent threat of violence or serious disorder.

The defendant, the Nevada Public Peace Commission, asserts that the Act is a necessary and reasonable regulation of public space to prevent the spread of dangerous or inflammatory rhetoric that could lead to unrest. The Commission argues that the Act is narrowly tailored, as it is designed to address specific threats to public safety and that its application is limited to instances where speech is likely to cause immediate disruption.

---

**III. LEGAL STANDARD**    

The First Amendment to the United States Constitution, as incorporated by the Fourteenth Amendment, prohibits the government from abridging the freedom of speech. *See* *West Virginia v. Barnette*, 319 U.S. 624 (1943). When evaluating the constitutionality of a law that regulates speech, the Supreme Court has established a rigorous standard of scrutiny. *See* *United States v. Grace*, 461 U.S. 171 (1983).

In the context of public spaces, the government may impose restrictions on speech if the law is content-neutral and leaves open ample alternative channels for communication. *See* *Hess v. Indiana*, 414 U.S. 105 (1973). However, when a law imposes content-based restrictions, the government must demonstrate that the regulation is narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest. *See* *Ward v. Rock Against Racism*, 491 U.S. 781 (1989).

The Supreme Court has long held that laws that restrict speech based on the content of the message are subject to the most exacting scrutiny. *See* *New York Times Co. v. Sullivan*, 376 U.S. 254 (1964). In such cases, the government must show that the regulation is not merely a means of suppressing disfavored viewpoints but is instead justified by a significant governmental interest and is narrowly tailored to avoid suppressing speech that is not harmful.

This Court must therefore determine whether the Act, as interpreted and applied by the Nevada Public Peace Commission, constitutes a content-based restriction on speech, and if so, whether it satisfies the narrow tailoring requirement under the First Amendment.

---

**Iv. Constitutional Analysis**

**A. The Act’s Content-Based Nature**

The central issue before this Court is whether the Act is a content-based regulation of speech. A law is content-based if it restricts speech based on the message it conveys. *See* *Hedges v. Florida*, 604 F.3d 1270 (11th Cir. 2010). In this case, the Act’s restriction on “controversial or disruptive content” is inherently content-based, as it is not limited to speech that is inherently dangerous or violent, but rather applies to a wide range of expressive activities that may be deemed controversial or provocative by the government.

Section 3 of the Act, which provides the statutory basis for the restriction, does not define “controversial or disruptive content” in any manner that is sufficiently precise to avoid content-based regulation. Instead, it leaves the determination to the discretion of the Nevada Public Peace Commission, which is tasked with evaluating the potential for “imminent threat to public order.” This broad standard allows the Commission to suppress speech based on its subjective assessment of whether a particular message is “controversial” or “disruptive,” rather than on any objective criterion of harm or risk.

The lack of definitional clarity in the Act raises serious constitutional concerns. The Supreme Court has consistently held that laws that define speech based on the content of the message, rather than on the context in which it is spoken, are subject to strict scrutiny. *See* *United States v. Grace*, 461 U.S. 171, 178 (1983). In the absence of a clear and precise definition of the speech that is restricted, the Act operates as a content-based regulation that is not narrowly tailored and is therefore constitutionally suspect.

**B. The Government’s Interest in Public Order**


The defendants argue that the Act serves a compelling governmental interest in preserving public order and safety. While the government undoubtedly has a compelling interest in maintaining public order, the Supreme Court has made it clear that this interest alone is insufficient to justify content-based restrictions on speech. *See* *United States v. Grace*, 461 U.S. 171, 182 (1983). The government must demonstrate that the restriction is narrowly tailored to serve that interest and does not suppress speech that is not directly connected to the threat of harm.

In this case, the Act’s broad application allows for the suppression of speech that may be controversial but not necessarily harmful. The lack of a clear standard for determining what constitutes an “imminent threat to public order” permits the Commission to suppress speech that may not pose any real or immediate danger. The absence of a specific threshold for identifying such threats means that the law is not narrowly tailored and is therefore not justified under the First Amendment.

**C. The Narrow Tailoring Requirement**

Under the First Amendment, a content-based regulation must be narrowly tailored to serve a significant governmental interest. *See* *Ward v. Rock Against Racism*, 491 U.S. 781, 791 (1989). To satisfy this requirement, the law must be the least restrictive means available to achieve the government’s objective. *See* *United States v. Grace*, 461 U.S. 171, 182 (1983).

The Act fails to meet this standard. The law prohibits a wide range of speech based on its content, without any specific limitation to speech that is likely to cause immediate harm. The absence of any clear definition of “controversial or disruptive content” means that the Act could be applied to suppress speech that is not directly related to the government’s interest in maintaining public order. The failure to define the scope of the restriction renders the Act overbroad and not narrowly tailored.

Furthermore, the Act’s application is not limited to specific contexts or locations. The Commission has the authority to issue directives to local jurisdictions, which may then enforce the Act in any public space. This lack of geographic or contextual specificity further undermines the Act’s narrow tailoring and raises the risk of overreach.

**D. The First Amendment’s Protection of Public Discourse**

The First Amendment protects the right to engage in public discourse, even in the face of potential controversy. *See* *New York Times Co. v. Sullivan*, 376 U.S. 254, 270 (1964). The Supreme Court has consistently held that the government may not suppress speech simply because it is controversial or provocative, unless it poses a direct threat to public safety. *See* *Hess v. Indiana*, 414 U.S. 105, 110–11 (1973).

In *Hess v. Indiana*, the Court struck down an Indiana law that prohibited the distribution of leaflets that “might cause a breach of the peace,” holding that such a law was overbroad and not narrowly tailored. *See* 414 U.S. at 110–11. Similarly, in *United States v. Grace*, the Court invalidated a law that prohibited the distribution of leaflets that “might cause a breach of the peace,” emphasizing that the law was not narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest. *See* 461 U.S. at 182.

The Act in this case is even more expansive in its application than the law at issue in *Hess v. Indiana* or *United States v. Grace*. It does not merely prohibit speech that might cause a breach of the peace, but instead restricts speech based on a subjective determination of whether it is “controversial” or “disruptive.” This broad standard allows for the suppression of a wide range of speech, even when no harm is imminent.

---

**V. THE ROLE OF THE NEDERLAND PUBLIC PEACE COMMISSION**

The Nevada Public Peace Commission, a body created by the Act, is tasked with enforcing the law’s restrictions on speech in public spaces. The Commission is empowered to issue public peace directives that guide local authorities in the enforcement of the Act. However, the Commission’s authority is not limited by any specific criteria or standards for determining what constitutes an “imminent threat to public order.” This lack of specificity allows for the Commission to apply the Act in a manner that is inconsistent with the First Amendment’s protection of free speech.

The Supreme Court has consistently held that the government may not use vague or overly broad standards to suppress speech. *See* *United States v. Grace*, 461 U.S. 171, 182 (1983). In this case, the Commission’s authority to issue directives based on a subjective assessment of whether speech is “controversial” or “disruptive” creates a significant risk of arbitrary enforcement and overreach. The lack of objective criteria for determining the content of speech that is restricted makes it impossible to assess whether the Act is narrowly tailored or whether it meets the standard of strict scrutiny required by the First Amendment.

---

**VI. CONCLUSION AND ORDER**

After a thorough examination of the facts, legal arguments, and constitutional principles at issue, this Court concludes that the *Nevada Public Peace Act of 2023* is a content-based regulation of speech that is not narrowly tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest. The Act’s broad application, lack of definitional clarity, and subjective criteria for determining what constitutes “controversial” or “disruptive” speech render it constitutionally suspect under the First Amendment.

The First Amendment protects the right to engage in public discourse, even in the face of controversy. The government may not suppress speech merely because it is controversial or provocative, unless it poses a direct threat to public safety. The Act in this case fails to meet that standard and is therefore unconstitutional as applied.

Therefore, the Court finds that the *Nevada Public Peace Act of 2023* is void as applied to the plaintiff and all similarly situated speakers. The Act is invalid under the First Amendment and cannot be enforced in any public space without violating the constitutional rights of those who exercise their right to free speech.

IT IS ORDERED that the *Nevada Public Peace Act of 2023* is hereby struck down and declared unconstitutional as applied to the plaintiff and all similarly situated speakers. The defendants are enjoined from enforcing the Act in any public space, and any future attempts to do so are hereby prohibited.

The parties are directed to submit a status report within thirty (30) days of the date of this order, detailing any further legal proceedings or relief sought. The Court retains jurisdiction over this matter and may modify or vacate this order upon further notice or upon a showing of changed circumstances.

So ORDERED this 12th day of April, 2025.

**B. The Principle of Overbreadth**

The concept of overbreadth is a critical doctrine in First Amendment jurisprudence. A law is overbroad if it prohibits a substantial amount of protected speech, even if it may serve a legitimate governmental interest. *See* *United States v. Grace*, 461 U.S. 171, 183 (1983). The Supreme Court has held that overbroad laws are invalid even if they are not applied in a discriminatory manner, because they chill the exercise of constitutional rights.

In this case, the *Nevada Public Peace Act* is overbroad in several respects. First, it applies to a wide range of expressive activities, including peaceful protests, political discourse, and academic debate, all of which are protected by the First Amendment. Second, the Act does not provide any specific criteria for determining what constitutes an “imminent threat to public order,” leaving the determination to the subjective discretion of the Nevada Public Peace Commission. This lack of clarity creates a significant risk of overreach and arbitrary enforcement.

Furthermore, the Act’s language is vague and lacks precision. The term “controversial” is not defined, and the term “disruptive” is similarly undefined. This vagueness makes it impossible for speakers to know what conduct is prohibited, thereby chilling the exercise of free speech. *See* *New York Times Co. v. Sullivan*, 376 U.S. 254, 270 (1964). The Supreme Court has held that laws that fail to provide sufficient notice of their prohibitions are unconstitutional under the First Amendment.
