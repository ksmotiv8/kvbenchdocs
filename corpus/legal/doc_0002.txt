**MOTION TO DISMISS FOR LACK OF JURISDICTION**  

This Motion to Dismiss for Lack of Jurisdiction is filed by the Defendant, BlueWave Industries, LLC, pursuant to the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, specifically Rule 12(b)(1), and is based upon the absence of subject matter jurisdiction over the claims asserted by the Plaintiff, Greenfield Enterprises, Inc. The Defendant respectfully moves this Court to dismiss the entire complaint for lack of jurisdiction, both on the grounds of complete absence of jurisdiction and the failure of the Plaintiff to establish a jurisdictional basis that meets the requirements of 28 U.S.C. § 1331, 28 U.S.C. § 1332, or any other applicable federal statute.  

---

### I. INTRODUCTION

This motion arises from the Complaint filed by Greenfield Enterprises, Inc., a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in the State of California, against BlueWave Industries, LLC, a Nevada corporation with its principal place of business in the State of Texas. The Complaint alleges a variety of claims, including breach of contract, tortious interference with business relations, and unjust enrichment, all of which are rooted in state law. The Plaintiff, however, has attempted to assert jurisdiction over the Defendant in this federal court by invoking the general federal question jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1331 and by claiming that the amount in controversy exceeds the jurisdictional threshold of $75,000.  

The Defendant, however, contends that neither of these bases for federal jurisdiction is valid. First, the claims asserted by the Plaintiff are not based upon a federal question, but rather are grounded in the laws of the State of Texas, which governs the parties’ contractual and tortious relationships. Second, the amount in controversy, as alleged, does not meet the jurisdictional threshold required under 28 U.S.C. § 1331. Moreover, the Plaintiff has failed to allege that the matter involves a substantial federal question that justifies federal jurisdiction.  


The Defendant further argues that the Complaint fails to establish the necessary diversity of citizenship required for diversity jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1332. Specifically, the Plaintiff has not alleged that the Defendant is a citizen of a state other than California, and the Defendant is a Nevada corporation with its principal place of business in Texas, which may not be sufficient to establish complete diversity.  

The Defendant is therefore compelled to move this Court to dismiss the entire Complaint for lack of jurisdiction, as the Court lacks the authority to adjudicate the claims asserted by the Plaintiff.  

---

### II. FACTUAL AND PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

The parties are corporate entities operating in the technology and energy sectors, with Greenfield Enterprises, Inc., being a California-based company that has engaged in a series of business dealings with BlueWave Industries, LLC, a Nevada-based entity. The relationship between the two companies has been ongoing for several years, with BlueWave Industries, LLC, providing certain services to Greenfield Enterprises, Inc., under a series of contractual agreements.  

The Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that the Defendant has breached the terms of these agreements and has engaged in tortious conduct that has caused the Plaintiff substantial financial loss. The Complaint also claims that the Defendant has interfered with the Plaintiff’s business relations and has unjustly enriched itself at the Plaintiff’s expense.  

The Plaintiff, in its Complaint, asserts that the federal court has jurisdiction over the case because the claims are based upon a federal question. The Plaintiff also alleges that the amount in controversy exceeds the jurisdictional threshold under 28 U.S.C. § 1331. The Plaintiff further claims that the Defendant is a citizen of a state other than California, thereby satisfying the diversity requirements of 28 U.S.C. § 1332.  

The Defendant, however, contends that the allegations in the Complaint are entirely based upon state law and that the federal court lacks subject matter jurisdiction. The Defendant also contends that the amount in controversy is not sufficiently established to justify federal jurisdiction. Moreover, the Defendant asserts that the Plaintiff has failed to establish that the Defendant is a citizen of a state other than California, thereby failing to meet the requirements for diversity jurisdiction.  

---

### III. LEGAL STANDARD

Under Rule 12(b)(1) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, a defendant may move to dismiss a complaint for lack of subject matter jurisdiction. The burden of establishing jurisdiction rests with the plaintiff, and the court may examine the pleadings, affidavits, and other evidence to determine whether jurisdiction exists.  

The standard for determining jurisdiction is strict and requires the plaintiff to demonstrate not only that the court has the power to adjudicate the case, but also that the case falls within the scope of that power. The court may consider the allegations in the complaint, but may also look beyond the pleadings to determine whether jurisdiction exists.  

In the case of a motion to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction, the court may not consider the truth of the allegations but may consider the legal sufficiency of the jurisdictional basis. The motion may also be granted if the court finds that the jurisdictional requirements have not been met.  

In the absence of a valid basis for jurisdiction, the court may dismiss the case with prejudice or without prejudice, depending on the circumstances.

### IV. ARGUMENT


#### A. The Claims in the Complaint Are Grounded in State Law and Are Not Based Upon a Federal Question

The Plaintiff’s Complaint is entirely based upon the laws of the State of Texas, which governs the contractual and tortious relationships between the parties. The claims of breach of contract, tortious interference with business relations, and unjust enrichment are all state law claims that do not involve any federal question.  

Under 28 U.S.C. § 1331, federal courts have jurisdiction over civil actions arising under the Constitution, laws, or treaties of the United States. However, this jurisdiction is limited to cases that are based upon federal law or that involve a substantial federal question. The claims asserted by the Plaintiff are not based upon any federal law, nor do they involve a substantial federal question.  

The Plaintiff’s argument that the claims are based upon a federal question is therefore baseless. The claims are purely state law claims that do not require the application of federal law. Therefore, the federal court lacks subject matter jurisdiction over the case.  

#### B. The Amount in Controversy Does Not Meet the Jurisdictional Threshold Under 28 U.S.C. § 1331

The Plaintiff also asserts that the amount in controversy exceeds the jurisdictional threshold of $75,000 under 28 U.S.C. § 1331. However, the Complaint does not provide any specific figures or evidence to support this assertion. The Plaintiff has merely alleged that the amount in controversy is substantial and that it exceeds the jurisdictional threshold.  

Under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, the plaintiff must provide a specific amount in controversy to support a claim under 28 U.S.C. § 1331. The Court may not assume that the amount in controversy is satisfied merely by the plaintiff’s allegations. The burden is on the plaintiff to demonstrate that the amount in controversy meets the jurisdictional threshold.  

In the absence of specific allegations or evidence that the amount in controversy exceeds $75,000, the court may not find that the jurisdictional threshold has been met. Therefore, the federal court lacks subject matter jurisdiction over the case.  

#### C. The Plaintiff Has Failed to Establish Diversity of Citizenship Required for Diversity Jurisdiction

The Plaintiff also asserts that the case is subject to diversity jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1332. However, the Complaint does not provide sufficient information to establish that the parties are citizens of different states.  

The Court must determine whether the parties are citizens of different states, and if so, whether the amount in controversy exceeds $75,000. The Plaintiff has failed to allege that the Defendant is a citizen of a state other than California, and the Defendant is a Nevada corporation with its principal place of business in Texas. Therefore, the Plaintiff has not satisfied the diversity requirement.  

The Court may not infer that the Defendant is a citizen of a state other than California merely because the Defendant is a Nevada corporation. The Court must determine the actual citizenship of the Defendant based on the allegations in the Complaint and any supporting evidence.  

#### D. The Court Lacks Jurisdiction to Adjudicate the Claims

In conclusion, the Court lacks subject matter jurisdiction over the claims asserted by the Plaintiff. The claims are based upon state law and do not involve any federal question. The amount in controversy has not been established, and the Plaintiff has failed to demonstrate that the parties are citizens of different states. Therefore, the federal court lacks jurisdiction over the case.  

The Defendant respectfully moves this Court to dismiss the entire Complaint for lack of jurisdiction, and to dismiss the case with prejudice.
