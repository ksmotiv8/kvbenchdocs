**II. THE WITNESS AND THE CONTEXT OF THE DEPOSITION**

The witness, *Jane Doe*, is a former sales associate at AutoSafe Motors, a used car dealership located in the City of Santa Monica. She was employed by the company from June 2022 through December 2023. Her position was primarily focused on customer service and vehicle inspection, although she was not authorized to perform mechanical inspections or provide technical assessments of the vehicles she sold.

The deposition was conducted as part of the discovery process in this personal injury case, where John Reynolds, the plaintiff, alleges that he suffered serious personal injuries as a result of an accident that occurred on March 12, 2023, while driving a used vehicle purchased from AutoSafe Motors. The vehicle in question, a 2018 Honda Civic, was allegedly defective and unroadworthy at the time of purchase, with undisclosed mechanical issues that contributed to the accident.

The deposition of Jane Doe was taken by counsel for John Reynolds, and the testimony includes a series of statements that, upon closer examination, reveal inconsistencies in the witness’s account of events. These inconsistencies pertain to the vehicle’s condition at the time of sale, the extent of the inspection process, and the communication between the sales team and customers regarding vehicle safety.

**III. THE NARRATIVE OF THE DEPOSITION**

**3.1. The Initial Statement of the Witness**

In her initial testimony, Jane Doe stated that she had no direct involvement in the sale of the 2018 Honda Civic to John Reynolds. She claimed that she was not assigned to the sales floor during the time of the sale and did not have any knowledge of the vehicle’s condition. She further asserted that the sales team at AutoSafe Motors conducted a standard inspection process for all vehicles, which included a visual check, a test drive, and a review of the vehicle’s history. She stated that all vehicles were deemed safe for sale unless there was a known mechanical issue that required disclosure to the customer.

**3.2. The Statement  Regarding the Inspection Process**

When asked about the inspection process, Jane Doe referenced Section 2.3 of the company’s internal policy manual, which outlines the procedures for vehicle inspections. She stated that the process included a "basic visual and mechanical check," which was conducted by the sales team. However, when pressed further, she admitted that the process was not standardized and that the level of inspection varied depending on the vehicle and the salesperson. She also conceded that the inspection did not include the use of diagnostic equipment or a comprehensive mechanical evaluation.

**3.3. Contradictions in the Witness’s Statement Regarding the 2018 Honda Civic**

The contradictions in the witness’s testimony became apparent when counsel for the plaintiff inquired about the specific vehicle in question. The witness initially stated that she had no knowledge of the 2018 Honda Civic and that it was not part of her responsibilities. However, when asked whether she had ever seen the vehicle or interacted with it in any capacity, she hesitated and then admitted that she had seen the vehicle in the showroom on the day of its sale. She stated that she had noticed the vehicle for a brief period and that it appeared to be in good condition.

This admission directly contradicted her earlier statement that she had no involvement with the vehicle. Counsel for the plaintiff then pointed out this inconsistency and asked her to clarify her position. The witness attempted to reconcile her statements by claiming that she had not been assigned to the sales floor that day and had not been involved in the sale of the vehicle. However, when asked for specific details about the vehicle’s condition and whether she had seen any documentation related to it, she was unable to provide a consistent response.

**3.4. The Statement Regarding Documentation and Disclosure**

Jane Doe was also asked about the documentation process used by AutoSafe Motors to track vehicle conditions and any potential issues. She stated that the company maintained a digital database of all vehicle inspections, which was accessible to all sales team members. However, when asked to provide an example of such documentation, she was unable to locate any records related to the 2018 Honda Civic and stated that the database was not regularly updated. This created a further contradiction in her testimony, as it suggested that the company’s internal documentation system was either incomplete or unreliable.

**3.5. The Statement Regarding the Test Drive**

The witness also testified about the test drive process, stating that all vehicles were required to undergo a test drive as part of the sales process. She claimed that the test drive was an opportunity for the customer to assess the vehicle’s performance and condition. However, when asked about the specific test drive conducted with John Reynolds, she was unable to recall whether the vehicle was driven in the normal operating condition or whether any mechanical issues were observed during the drive.

This lack of recollection was particularly problematic when counsel for the plaintiff pointed out that the vehicle’s engine had been reported to have a known issue with the transmission, which was not disclosed to the customer. The witness denied any knowledge of such an issue, despite the fact that AutoSafe Motors had a policy in place for disclosing known mechanical problems.

**3.6. The Statement Regarding Communication with Customers**

The witness also testified about the company’s communication policies with customers. She stated that sales team members were required to disclose any known issues with the vehicle to the customer prior to the sale. However, when asked whether she had ever seen a customer informed about a mechanical issue, she was unable to provide a specific example. This created a discrepancy between her stated policy and her actual behavior, as it suggested that the company’s communication protocols may not have been uniformly followed.

**3.7. The Statement Regarding the Sales Team’s Training**

When questioned about the training provided to the sales team, Jane Doe stated that all employees received basic training on vehicle inspection procedures, but that there was no formal training on how to identify mechanical issues. She further stated that the company relied on the sales team to make subjective judgments about the vehicle’s condition based on their observations. This assertion contradicted her earlier testimony regarding the inspection process and the level of detail required in the inspection.

**3.8. The Statement Regarding the Company’s Responsibility**

The witness was also asked about the company’s responsibility to ensure the safety of the vehicles it sold. She stated that AutoSafe Motors had a duty to provide customers with accurate information about the vehicles they sold, but that the company did not have a legal obligation to disclose all potential mechanical issues. She further claimed that the company was not liable for any defects that were not disclosed by the customer or that were not identified during the inspection process.

This statement was particularly problematic, as it suggested that the company’s liability was limited to the disclosure of known issues, which is a position that is not supported by the California Civil Code. Counsel for the plaintiff pointed out that the company’s liability under California law is broader, as it may be held responsible for defects that are not disclosed but are reasonably foreseeable.

**3.9. The Statement Regarding the Witness’s Knowledge of the Vehicle**

Throughout the deposition, the witness’s knowledge of the vehicle in question remained inconsistent. At times, she stated that she had no knowledge of the vehicle, while at other times, she admitted to having seen it in the showroom. However, when asked about her interactions with the vehicle, she was unable to provide a consistent account of her involvement.

This inconsistency in the witness’s testimony raises serious concerns about the reliability of her statements and the accuracy of her recollection of events. It also suggests that the company’s internal documentation and communication systems may have been inadequate or flawed.

**XV. RELEVANT LEGAL FRAMEWORK AND APPLICABLE LAWS**

**15.1. California Civil Code and Related Statutes**

The legal framework governing this case is primarily derived from the California Civil Code, particularly Sections 1542, 1543, and 1544, which outline the obligations of sellers and the rights of consumers in personal injury cases involving defective products. Section 1542 of the California Civil Code mandates that sellers must disclose any material defects in the goods they sell, and failure to do so may result in liability for the seller.
