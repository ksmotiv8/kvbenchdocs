**Plaintiff, Greenfield Enterprises, Inc.,** by and through undersigned counsel, hereby moves this Court for an order dismissing this action, and all claims asserted in the Complaint for want of subject matter jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1331 and 28 U.S.C. § 1332, and for any other relief this Court deems just and proper.  

---

**I. INTRODUCTION**  

This motion is brought by the Defendant, BlueWave Industries, LLC, in response to the Complaint filed by the Plaintiff, Greenfield Enterprises, Inc., which seeks to assert jurisdiction in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia. The Complaint, as set forth in its entirety, is predicated upon a claim that the Defendant has engaged in various wrongful acts and omissions in the course of its business dealings with the Plaintiff, and that these acts have occurred in a manner sufficient to establish federal jurisdiction under the diversity jurisdiction provisions of 28 U.S.C. § 1332.  

However, upon a close and thorough review of the allegations and the facts set forth in the Complaint, it becomes abundantly clear that the Court lacks the necessary jurisdiction to entertain this action. The assertion of jurisdiction by the Plaintiff is not only legally unsound but is also factually unsupported. The Court’s jurisdiction is derived from two primary sources: (1) the federal question jurisdiction granted by 28 U.S.C. § 1331, and (2) the diversity jurisdiction granted by 28 U.S.C. § 1332. Neither of these sources is applicable to the claims in this action.  

The Complaint does not, in fact, present any federal question that is within the purview of this Court. Instead, the claims asserted by the Plaintiff are rooted entirely in state law, and thus, the Court does not have the authority to entertain them under the federal question jurisdiction. Furthermore, the diversity jurisdiction is also not applicable, as the parties are not of diverse citizenship, and the amount in controversy does not satisfy the statutory threshold.  

Therefore, this Court is without jurisdiction over this action, and accordingly, the entire Complaint must be dismissed, and the case must be remanded to the appropriate state court.  

---

**II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND**  

The factual allegations in the Complaint, as presented by the Plaintiff, are as follows:  

Greenfield Enterprises, Inc., a company headquartered in the State of Georgia, is engaged in the manufacturing and distribution of industrial equipment and machinery. BlueWave Industries, LLC, a Delaware limited liability company, is a company based in the State of Texas. According to the Complaint, BlueWave Industries, LLC, has engaged in various acts of misconduct, including breach of contract, fraudulent misrepresentation, and deceptive trade practices, in its dealings with Greenfield Enterprises, Inc.  

The Complaint asserts that these acts have occurred primarily in the State of Texas, and that as a result, the Court of the United States for the District of Columbia has jurisdiction over the matter under the doctrine of forum selection clauses, or alternatively, under the diversity jurisdiction. The Plaintiff further contends that the amount in controversy exceeds $75,000, and that the parties are of diverse citizenship.  

However, a review of the allegations in the Complaint reveals that the Plaintiff has failed to establish either a federal question or diversity jurisdiction. The claims are based entirely on state law, and the parties are not of diverse citizenship. Moreover, the allegations do not indicate that the acts giving rise to the claims occurred in the District of Columbia, nor that the Court has any connection to the subject matter of the litigation.  

Furthermore, the Complaint does not contain any mention of any federal statute, regulation, or constitutional provision upon which the Plaintiff seeks to base its claims. The claims are all grounded in state law, and therefore, the federal question jurisdiction does not apply.  

As for diversity jurisdiction, the Plaintiff has not demonstrated that the parties are of diverse citizenship. The Plaintiff is a Georgia corporation, and the Defendant is a Delaware limited liability company with its principal place of business in Texas. Therefore, the citizenship of both parties is based on their respective states of incorporation, and they are not of diverse citizenship.  

Moreover, the amount in controversy is not clearly established. While the Plaintiff states that the amount in controversy exceeds $75,000, it does not provide any supporting evidence, and the allegations do not sufficiently indicate the actual value of the claims. Therefore, the diversity jurisdiction is not satisfied.

**III. LEGAL STANDARD**  

A motion to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction is a matter of law and must be decided by the Court upon a review of the pleadings and any supporting materials. Under Rule 12(b)(1) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, a party may move to dismiss an action for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.  

The burden of proving the existence of jurisdiction rests upon the plaintiff, and the plaintiff must demonstrate that jurisdiction exists in order for the action to proceed. If the Court finds that the plaintiff has failed to establish jurisdiction, then the entire action must be dismissed, and the case must be remanded to the appropriate state court.  

The determination of subject matter jurisdiction is a question of law, and the Court must examine the pleadings to determine whether the plaintiff has presented a claim that falls within the Court’s jurisdiction. If the pleadings fail to demonstrate that the Court has jurisdiction, the Court must dismiss the action.  

In addition to the burden of proof, the Court may also consider the adequacy of the plaintiff’s allegations. If the plaintiff’s claims are based on state law, then the Court does not have jurisdiction unless the claim is grounded in a federal question or the parties are of diverse citizenship. If the plaintiff has not demonstrated that the Court has jurisdiction, then the Court must dismiss the action.

**IV. ARGUMENT**  

**A. Lack of Federal Question Jurisdiction**  

The first and most fundamental issue in this case is whether the Court has federal question jurisdiction. Under 28 U.S.C. § 1331, federal question jurisdiction exists when the claim is based on a federal statute, regulation, or constitutional provision.  

In the present case, the Plaintiff has failed to assert any claim that is based on federal law. All of the claims set forth in the Complaint are based on state law, and none of the allegations relate to any federal statute, regulation, or constitutional provision.  

The allegations in the Complaint are entirely state law-based. The Plaintiff asserts claims for breach of contract, fraudulent misrepresentation, and deceptive trade practices, all of which are governed by the laws of the State of Texas and the State of Georgia. These claims do not involve any federal statutes, regulations, or constitutional provisions. Therefore, the federal question jurisdiction does not apply.  

Furthermore, the Plaintiff has not alleged any facts that would establish that the Court has federal question jurisdiction. The allegations are entirely based on state law, and the Court has no jurisdiction over claims that are grounded in state law unless the claim is brought under a federal statute or the parties are of diverse citizenship.  

Therefore, the Court does not have federal question jurisdiction, and the entire action must be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction.  

**B.  Lack of Diversity Jurisdiction**  

The second issue in this case is whether the Court has diversity jurisdiction. Under 28 U.S.C. § 1332, diversity jurisdiction exists when the parties are citizens of different states and the amount in controversy exceeds $75,000.  

The Plaintiff is a Georgia corporation, and the Defendant is a Delaware limited liability company with its principal place of business in Texas. Therefore, the parties are not of diverse citizenship. The Plaintiff is a Georgia corporation, and the Defendant is a Delaware company, and thus, they are not citizens of different states.  

Furthermore, the amount in controversy is not clearly established. While the Plaintiff states that the amount in controversy exceeds $75,000, it does not provide any supporting evidence. The allegations in the Complaint do not clearly indicate the actual value of the claims. Therefore, the diversity jurisdiction is not satisfied.  

Moreover, the Court must also consider whether the parties are actually citizens of the states they claim to be citizens of. In determining citizenship for the purposes of diversity jurisdiction, the Court must look to the state of incorporation and the principal place of business.  

The Plaintiff is a Georgia corporation, and the Defendant is a Delaware company with its principal place of business in Texas. Therefore, the parties are not citizens of different states. The Defendant is a Delaware company, and thus, its citizenship is based on its state of incorporation, not its principal place of business.  

Therefore, the parties are not of diverse citizenship, and the diversity jurisdiction is not applicable.  

**C. Forum Selection Clauses and Venue**  

The Plaintiff has also attempted to assert jurisdiction on the grounds of forum selection clauses and venue. However, the Court must consider whether the forum selection clause is valid and whether the Plaintiff has demonstrated that the Court has jurisdiction under such clauses.  

The Complaint does not mention any forum selection clause. Therefore, the Court must conclude that the Plaintiff has failed to assert any such clause as a basis for jurisdiction.  

Furthermore, the Court must also consider whether the Plaintiff has established that the forum selection clause is valid. The validity of a forum selection clause depends on whether it is enforceable under the law of the state where the contract was formed and whether it is enforceable under the law of the forum.  

The Plaintiff has not provided any evidence that a forum selection clause exists, nor has it provided any evidence that such a clause is valid. Therefore, the Court must conclude that the forum selection clause is not applicable to this case.  

Additionally, the Court must consider whether the venue is appropriate. The venue is determined based on the location of the defendant’s principal place of business or where the contract was executed. However, the Complaint does not indicate that the defendant’s principal place of business is in the District of Columbia. Therefore, the venue is not appropriate, and the Court does not have jurisdiction based on venue.  

Therefore, the Court does not have jurisdiction based on forum selection clauses or venue.  

---

**V. CONCLUSION**  

In conclusion, the Court has reviewed the allegations and the facts set forth in the Complaint and has determined that the Court lacks jurisdiction over this action. The claims asserted by the Plaintiff are entirely based on state law, and the parties are not of diverse citizenship. Additionally, the amount in controversy is not clearly established, and the forum selection clause is not applicable.  

Therefore, the Court must dismiss this action for lack of jurisdiction. The entire action must be dismissed, and the case must be remanded to the appropriate state court.
