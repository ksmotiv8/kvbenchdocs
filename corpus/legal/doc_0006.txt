**I. INTRODUCTION**  

This appellate brief is submitted on behalf of the Appellant, TaxConsultants LLC (“TaxConsultants”), in the above-captioned matter. The case arises from a complex tax dispute concerning the interpretation of a federal statute known as the *Federal Revenue Code of 2012* (the “Code”), specifically Section 114.5, titled “Special Tax Treatment for Certain Professional Services Rendered to Governmental Entities.” The dispute centers on the lower court’s application of this provision in the context of a contractual arrangement between TaxConsultants and the Federal Revenue Authority (the “FRA”), a quasi-judicial agency responsible for the assessment and collection of federal taxes.  

The lower court, in its decision dated March 12, 2024, ruled that the FRA had properly interpreted Section 114.5 to require TaxConsultants to remit a 15% additional tax on all professional services rendered to governmental entities, regardless of the nature or scope of such services. This determination, while grounded in a literal reading of the statute’s text, fails to account for the legislative history, the broader policy framework of the Code, and the intent of the statute’s drafting body.  

The Appellant contends that the lower court’s interpretation is not only legally erroneous but also inconsistent with the principles of statutory construction, particularly the canons of ejusdem generis, the doctrine of noscitur a sociis, and the maxim that statutes should be interpreted in a manner that promotes their general purpose. Moreover, the Appellant argues that the lower court’s approach disregards the statutory definitions, the legislative history, and the structural coherence of the Code as a whole.  

This appeal presents an important question of statutory interpretation: When a federal statute employs a broad or ambiguous term, particularly in the context of a specific statutory provision, how should a court balance the text of the statute against the legislative intent, the structure of the Code, and the broader policy objectives that underpin its enactment? This question is of first impression in this jurisdiction and requires a careful and principled analysis of the statutory language, its context, and its intended effect.  

---

**II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND**  

TaxConsultants, a registered professional services firm specializing in tax advisory and consulting services, entered into a multi-year contract with the FRA in 2019. Under the terms of the agreement, TaxConsultants provided various services, including tax planning, compliance review, and policy analysis, to multiple federal agencies and departments, including the Department of Treasury, the Internal Revenue Service, and the Office of Management and Budget.  

In 2022, the FRA issued a notice of assessment, asserting that TaxConsultants had failed to comply with Section 114.5 of the Code, which provides for a special tax treatment for certain professional services rendered to governmental entities. The FRA’s notice alleged that all services provided by TaxConsultants to any federal agency were subject to a 15% additional tax, regardless of the specific nature of the service, the client’s status, or the contractual terms of the agreement.  

TaxConsultants challenged the assessment, arguing that the language of Section 114.5 was both ambiguous and overly broad, and that the FRA’s interpretation failed to account for the statutory definitions, the legislative intent, and the broader policy objectives of the Code. The lower court, however, ruled in favor of the FRA, concluding that the term “professional services” in Section 114.5 was to be interpreted in its broadest possible sense, and that the phrase “rendered to governmental entities” encompassed all services provided to any federal agency, regardless of the nature or context.  

This determination was not based on any particular statutory definition, but rather on a literal reading of the text, which the lower court characterized as “plain and unambiguous.” The Appellant contends that this approach is legally flawed and inconsistent with established principles of statutory construction.  

---

**III. STANDARD OF REVIEW**  

In reviewing the lower court’s interpretation of the Code, this Court is bound by the standard of review articulated in *Mandate v. Federal Tax Appeals Board*, 2013 WL 1234567 (9th Cir. 2013), which holds that when a statute is ambiguous, and the lower court has applied a rule of statutory construction that is not grounded in the text, context, or legislative intent, the appellate court may reverse such a determination if it is clearly erroneous.  

The lower court’s interpretation of Section 114.5 is not merely ambiguous; it is legally and factually unsupported. The Appellant’s motion for reversal is grounded in the assertion that the lower court failed to consider the statutory definitions, the legislative history, the structure of the Code, and the policy objectives that underpin its enactment.  

This Court has consistently held that when a statute employs a term that is susceptible to multiple interpretations, the court must look beyond the text itself and consider the legislative intent, the structure of the Code, and the context in which the provision was enacted. *See*, e.g., *Kline v. Internal Revenue Appeals Board*, 2018 WL 1234567 (9th Cir. 2018) (“A court must not interpret a statute in a manner that ignores its structure, its purpose, or the broader statutory framework.”).  

In this case, the lower court’s literalist interpretation of Section 114.5 is not only legally unsound, but it also disregards the very principles of statutory construction that this Court has repeatedly emphasized. The Appellant’s motion for reversal is therefore both warranted and necessary to correct the lower court’s legal error.

 **IV. LEGAL ARGUMENT**  

**A. The Text of Section 114.5 Is Ambiguous and Must Be Interpreted in Context**  

Section 114.5 of the Code provides as follows:  

> “Section 114.5. Special Tax Treatment for Certain Professional Services Rendered to Governmental Entities.  
> (a) In general. For any taxable year, any individual or entity that provides professional services to any governmental entity, as defined in Section 114.1, shall be subject to an additional tax of 15% of the gross revenue derived from such services.  
> (b) Definitions. As used in this section:  
> (1) “Governmental entity” means any federal, state, or local governmental body or agency.  
> (2) “Professional services” means any services rendered by an individual or entity that require specialized knowledge or expertise in the field of taxation, accounting, or financial planning.”  

The Appellant’s contention is that the lower court’s interpretation of this provision is legally unsound because it applies a literal reading of the term “professional services” without regard to the statutory definitions, the legislative history, or the structure of the Code.  

The term “professional services” is not defined in the Code, but it is defined in Section 114.1, which is cited in Section 114.5 as the source of the term. Section 114.1 provides:  

> “Section 114.1. Definitions.  
> (a) As used in this subchapter:  
> (1) ‘Governmental entity’ means any federal, state, or local governmental body or agency.  
> (2) ‘Professional services’ means any services rendered by an individual or entity that require specialized knowledge or expertise in the field of taxation, accounting, or financial planning.  
> (3) ‘Gross revenue’ means the total amount of income received by an individual or entity from the rendering of professional services, excluding any deductions or credits permitted by this subchapter.”  

The Appellant’s argument is that the lower court failed to consider the statutory definition of “professional services” in Section 114.1, which is directly referenced in Section 114.5. By failing to incorporate this definition into its interpretation of the term “professional services” in Section 114.5, the lower court has engaged in an error of statutory construction.  

Moreover, the Appellant contends that the lower court’s interpretation is inconsistent with the structure of the Code as a whole. The Code is a comprehensive tax code that includes numerous provisions governing the taxation of professional services, and it is structured to apply different tax rates and rules based on the nature of the service, the type of client, and the jurisdiction of the service provider.  

The lower court’s interpretation, which applies a uniform 15% tax to all services rendered to governmental entities, ignores this structural coherence. Instead, it adopts a blanket application of the tax that is not supported by the statutory text, the legislative intent, or the policy objectives of the Code.  

**B. The Legislative Intent Behind Section 114.5 Is Not To Tax All Professional Services Rendered to Governmental Entities**  

The Appellant’s motion for reversal is also supported by the legislative history of Section 114.5. The provision was enacted in 2012 as part of a broader legislative effort to increase revenue by imposing a special tax on professional services rendered to governmental entities. However, the legislative intent was not to impose a tax on all such services, but rather to target those services that were deemed to be of a particular type or nature.  

The legislative history of Section 114.5 is detailed in the Congressional Record, which indicates that the provision was intended to apply to services that were of a commercial or for-profit nature, rather than to services that were of a governmental or public service nature.  

For example, the House Report accompanying the 2012 tax reform package states:  

> “Section 114.5 is intended to apply to professional services that are of a commercial or for-profit nature. The provision is not intended to apply to services provided by governmental entities to other governmental entities, nor to services that are of a public service nature.”  

The Appellant argues that the lower court ignored this legislative intent and applied the provision in a manner that is inconsistent with the purpose for which it was enacted.  


**C. The Lower Court’s Interpretation Is Not Supported by the Statutory Definitions or the Structure of the Code**  

As previously noted, the lower court’s interpretation of Section 114.5 is not supported by the statutory definitions provided in Section 114.1. The term “professional services” is defined in Section 114.1 as “any services rendered by an individual or entity that require specialized knowledge or expertise in the field of taxation, accounting, or financial planning.”  

The Appellant argues that this definition is not broad enough to include all professional services, and that the lower court’s interpretation of the term is inconsistent with the statutory text.  

Furthermore, the Appellant contends that the lower court’s interpretation is inconsistent with the structure of the Code as a whole. The Code is a comprehensive tax code that includes numerous provisions governing the taxation of professional services, and it is structured to apply different tax rates and rules based on the nature of the service, the type of client, and the jurisdiction of the service provider.  

The lower court’s interpretation, which applies a uniform 15% tax to all services rendered to governmental entities, ignores this structural coherence. Instead, it adopts a blanket application of the tax that is not supported by the statutory text, the legislative intent, or the policy objectives of the Code.  

**D. The Lower Court’s Interpretation Is Inconsistent with the Doctrine of Statutory Construction**  

The Appellant’s motion for reversal is also supported by the doctrine of statutory construction. This doctrine holds that when a statute is ambiguous, a court must interpret it in a manner that promotes its general purpose and that is consistent with the broader statutory framework.  

In this case, the lower court’s interpretation of Section 114.5 is not only ambiguous, but it is also inconsistent with the purpose and structure of the Code. The provision was enacted to target certain professional services, not all professional services, and the lower court’s interpretation ignores this fact.  

The Appellant contends that the lower court’s interpretation is not supported by the text of the statute, the legislative history, or the structure of the Code. Therefore, it is legally unsound and must be reversed.  

**E. The Lower Court’s Interpretation Fails to Account for the Policy Objectives of the Code**  

The Appellant’s motion for reversal is also supported by the policy objectives of the Code. The Code is a comprehensive tax code that includes numerous provisions governing the taxation of professional services, and it is structured to apply different tax rates and rules based on the nature of the service, the type of client, and the jurisdiction of the service provider.  

The lower court’s interpretation of Section 114.5 is not only inconsistent with the structure of the Code, but it also fails to account for the policy objectives that underpin its enactment. The provision was enacted to increase revenue by imposing a special tax on professional services rendered to governmental entities, but it was not intended to apply to all such services.  

The Appellant argues that the lower court’s interpretation is not only legally unsound, but it is also inconsistent with the policy objectives of the Code. Therefore, it must be reversed.
