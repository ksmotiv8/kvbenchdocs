Bonsai 1-bit and Gemma 4

Latency is the binding constraint for systems that use LLMs as components. These are the fastest models I could run on a single L4, what the numbers mean, and how they change what you can build.

Thesis

Latency is a constraint, not a metric

Latency is not just a number on a dashboard. It is a constraint that determines what systems are buildable. At 2 seconds per response you can build a chatbot. At 500 milliseconds you can put an LLM in the middle of a web request path. At 200 milliseconds you can chain multiple LLM calls inside a single user interaction without anything feeling slow.

For a 50-token response, that maps to roughly 40 ms/tok, 10 ms/tok, and 4 ms/tok respectively. Most of the models in this comparison sit in the 19-21 ms/tok band. One model sits below 10. None sit below 4.

This post tests how low you can go on a single L4 GPU and what becomes possible at each level. I started with a narrow question (can Bonsai's 1-bit weights actually deliver the speed the paper claims?) and the scope expanded as I went. It now covers Bonsai 1-bit 8B, Gemma 4 E2B and E4B, Qwen 2.5 7B AWQ, Qwen 3.5 9B in BF16 and AWQ, GPT-OSS 20B, and GLM 4.7 Flash AWQ.

Observations

What the numbers are telling us

Bonsai 1-bit 8B is the only model under 10 ms/tok. 8.4 ms/tok is real wall-clock time on the L4, not a projection or a decode-rate-during-thinking number. It scored 82.8% on IFEval (second only to Gemma 4 E4B at 86.9% among non-reasoning models) and emits raw JSON and executable code without wrapping them in markdown fences. The 1-bit weights are 1.15 GB on disk against the 5.2 GB INT4 Qwen and 9.5 GB BF16 Gemma E2B it competes with, which is the mechanical reason for the speed. There is a real cost: Bonsai scored 68.9% on GSM8K against the 93.0% its base model (Qwen3-8B) reportedly hits unquantized. That is a 24-point regression on math reasoning. The 1-bit format is excellent for short-form structured output and noticeably weaker on multi-step arithmetic.

Qwen 2.5 7B AWQ is the practical baseline. Mature SGLang runtime, CUDA graphs enabled, INT4 quantization, 19.1 ms/tok. It is the most boring choice and the most production-ready. Benchmark scores are lower than newer models (73.4% IFEval, 70.6% GSM8K), but the tooling is stable and the behavior is predictable. If you need something that works today and you are not chasing a specific quality bar, this is the default.

Gemma 4 E2B and E4B trade size for quality. E2B has 2.3B effective parameters and still scored 81.0% on IFEval, which surprised me. E4B at 4.5B effective doubled the cost to 40.5 ms/tok for 86.9% IFEval. Both models wrap their output in markdown, which limits their usefulness as pipeline components. They are good edge models and less ideal as system subroutines where something downstream is parsing the output.

Reasoning models hide their latency cost. GPT-OSS 20B, GLM 4.7 Flash AWQ, and Qwen 3.5 9B all score well on benchmarks (74 to 91%), and their visible decode rate clusters around 21 ms/tok. That number is misleading. With thinking enabled, the model spends most of its token budget inside a <think> block before any visible text appears. Real time-to-first-visible-token runs 5 to 50 times longer than the ms/tok figure suggests. GLM 4.7 Flash AWQ has the best math score in the comparison (91.3% GSM8K) and pays a latency cost users will notice.

Quality

Quality checks

I ran a 12-prompt manual quality test, the standard IFEval (541 prompts), and GSM8K (1319 problems for non-reasoning models, 150 for the reasoning models due to time constraints). The detailed per-prompt outputs and the full IFEval/GSM8K table are on the validation page. Three findings from the manual test deserve a mention here:

The AWQ acronym question was the sharpest discriminator. I asked every model "what does AWQ stand for in the context of LLM quantization?" The correct answer is "Activation-aware Weight Quantization." Three models got it wrong: Bonsai said "Approximate," Qwen 2.5 said "Adaptive," GPT-OSS 20B said "Adaptive." Three got it right: Gemma 4 E2B, Gemma 4 E4B, and GLM 4.7 Flash AWQ. The split does not correlate cleanly with size, quantization, or generation. It is just a useful reminder that "knows the right facts" is not a property that scales with parameter count.

Format compliance is binary in this set. When asked for raw JSON, Bonsai outputs {"name":"Qwen","language":"Chinese"}. Every other model wraps it in a markdown code fence (```json ... ```). When asked for an executable Python function, Bonsai outputs the function. The others wrap it in ```python ... ```. This was tested on a small number of prompts, so it is suggestive rather than definitive, but the pattern was consistent across the formatting prompts I ran.

GLM 4.7 Flash AWQ wins the formal benchmarks where it can. 91.3% on GSM8K is the best math result in the comparison and 5 points above the next best (GPT-OSS 20B at 86%). It also got the AWQ acronym right. The catch is that both numbers come from a reasoning model, which the latency section above explains is not what you want as a pipeline subroutine.

Implications

What this means for system design

Sub-200ms round trips change what you build. When inference is a subroutine instead of a service, you can chain calls. Classify the request, rewrite the prompt, generate the answer, verify the output. Four LLM calls at 150ms each completes in 600ms, which most users will not perceive as latency. The same chain at 1.5 seconds per call takes 6 seconds, which everyone perceives as broken. The threshold is not incremental.

Format compliance unblocks pipelining. A model that wraps JSON in markdown is friendly to humans reading a chat window and hostile to anything that needs to parse the output. If you are feeding the result into another system without a post-processing step, literal format compliance is more valuable than a few points of benchmark score. Bonsai and Qwen 2.5 win here. Gemma 4 does not.

Reasoning models are not low-latency models. They are quality-at-any-cost models. Their published benchmarks usually run with thinking disabled. Production serving with thinking on inflates visible response time by 5 to 50 times. If your system needs sub-second responses, a reasoning model is the wrong tool. If your system can wait 30 seconds for a high-quality answer and stream it as it arrives, they are excellent.

Recommendations

When to use which model

Bonsai 1-bit 8B: when latency is the constraint and the work is short-form. This is the model you pick when the LLM is a component in a larger system, not the end user's direct interface. It is fast enough to sit in a web request path, and it produces raw JSON and executable code without markdown wrapping in the prompts I tested. The factual gaps mean you would not ship it as a general-purpose assistant without retrieval, and the GSM8K regression means you should not rely on it for arithmetic. Good fit: classification, prompt rewriting, structured extraction, format normalization.

Gemma 4 E2B: when you need strong instruction-following at the smallest active parameter count. 2.3B effective parameters and 81% IFEval is impressive on paper. It still uses about 20 GB of VRAM on the L4 because of its embedding layout, so "edge" is relative. The markdown wrapping is an issue if you need machine-readable output, but for direct human interaction it is competitive with the larger models in the set.

Gemma 4 E4B: when quality matters more than latency and you are still on a single L4. Highest IFEval (86.9%) of any non-reasoning model in the comparison and better knowledge density than E2B, at roughly double the decode cost. The right pick when E2B is not quite good enough and you cannot move up to a larger GPU.

Qwen 2.5 7B AWQ: when you want the boring, stable, well-understood choice. Mature SGLang support, CUDA graphs, predictable behavior, and reasonable latency. The lower benchmark scores are real (it is an older generation), but nothing about deploying it surprises you. Reasonable default when stability matters more than benchmark headlines.

GLM 4.7 Flash AWQ: when you need the best quality and can tolerate multi-second responses. Highest math score in the comparison (91.3% GSM8K), highest non-reasoning IFEval among reasoning models (80.3%), got the AWQ acronym right. The catch: it is a reasoning model. The model spends most of its token budget inside <think> blocks before any visible token appears. In our 48-token latency test the visible content field was empty in every response. Real time-to-first-visible-token is several seconds. Use it when answer quality matters more than time-to-first-token.

GPT-OSS 20B: when you want the same reasoning model story with faster end-to-end response. Reasoning traces are 2-4x shorter than GLM's, so total wall-clock time is meaningfully better for the same quality bar. GSM8K 86%, IFEval 74.3%. Got the AWQ acronym wrong. Reasonable choice if GLM's latency profile is too variable for your use case but you still want reasoning-model quality on math.

Summary. Bonsai for short-form structured output. Gemma 4 E2B for instruction following at smaller active param count. Gemma 4 E4B when you need higher IFEval and can pay the latency cost. Qwen 2.5 7B AWQ as a stable production default. GLM 4.7 Flash AWQ when math quality matters and you can tolerate reasoning-model latency. GPT-OSS 20B as a faster-responding reasoning alternative. None of them is universally best.

Caveats

What this comparison is and is not

  • Each model runs on a different runtime (llama.cpp for Bonsai on PrismML's vendor fork, SGLang for Qwen and the reasoning models, vLLM for Gemma 4) with different optimization levels. Take the cross-model latency comparisons as directional, not absolute. Same-model-different-runtime tests would have isolated this confound. I could not run them because no single runtime supports all the model formats in this set.
  • All tests are single-request latency. Multi-user serving has different characteristics and different bottlenecks.
  • The 12-prompt manual quality test is small. The IFEval (541 prompts) and GSM8K (1319 problems) runs were full-suite for Bonsai, Qwen 2.5, Gemma 4 E2B, and Gemma 4 E4B. For the reasoning models (GPT-OSS 20B, GLM 4.7 Flash AWQ) they were limited to 150 prompts each due to time constraints. Apples-to-apples comparison between the reasoning and non-reasoning rows in the validation table is therefore approximate.
  • CUDA graphs are not supported for Gemma 4 on vLLM yet, so the Gemma 4 ms/tok numbers include extra kernel launch overhead that the other models avoid.
  • Reasoning model ms/tok numbers (GPT-OSS, GLM 4.7, Qwen 3.5) measure decode speed during the thinking phase, not the wall-clock time the user waits. In our 48-token latency test, the visible content field was empty for every reasoning-model response. Time-to-first-visible-token is several seconds for these models. See validation for the full discussion.
  • Qwen 3.5 9B IFEval and GSM8K results in the table are harness artifacts, not real capability. lm-eval's chat-completions backend cannot pass enable_thinking=false through the API, so the model burns its entire token budget inside <think> blocks and never reaches the answer. The Qwen 3.5 9B BF16 IFEval run was killed after roughly 12 hours of projected runtime to free the GPU for other tests.
  • Bonsai's 68.9% on GSM8K is a 24-point regression from the unquantized base model (Qwen3-8B at 93.0% per the Bonsai whitepaper). Aggressive quantization preserves instruction following far better than it preserves arithmetic.
What's next

Open questions

  • Gemma 4 MoE on H200. The 26B-A4B model with 3.8B active params per token is the most architecturally interesting for latency. It needs 48 GB VRAM at BF16. An H200 (141 GB HBM3e, 4.8 TB/s bandwidth) would give it room and speed. That is the next test I want to run.
  • Quantized Gemma 4. Running E4B at INT4 or FP8 would cut the model size and could significantly improve ms/tok. AWQ and GPTQ variants are starting to appear on HuggingFace but SGLang/vLLM support is still stabilizing.
  • Same-runtime comparison. Running Bonsai and Gemma 4 on the same engine would isolate the model-level latency difference from the runtime overhead. Neither engine supports both model formats today.
  • Longer outputs. All my tests used short outputs (10-48 tokens). Models behave differently on longer generations. Speculative decoding also becomes more relevant with longer outputs.
  • Bonsai vs Gemma 4 at same parameter count. Bonsai 4B vs Gemma 4 E4B (both ~4B effective) would be a fairer quality comparison.
